∎ § 01 the artifact

Erdős #1026, by hand.

What the four sentences were hiding.

The problem both solvers settled is Cambie’s conjecture, open since 1995:

erdős problem #1026 If x1, …, xk² are distinct positive reals with x1 + ⋯ + xk² = 1, some monotone subsequence has sum at least 1/k.

The AI prover Aristotle settled it first, autonomously, in 3,658 generated lines of Lean. Koishi Chan, a human reading the same problem list, posted four sentences an hour later. Most people read the episode as a story about machines. What interested me was what a precise reading of both proofs would surface.

So I formalized it the way a person would write it: about 330 lines of structured Lean 4 establishing the same theorem. Zero sorries, CI on every push, kernel-checked axiom audit. The Lean is the receipt. The deliverable is the reading around it:

  1. 01 A referee’s report on both informal proofs. Every gap, hidden assumption and unstated lemma, flagged and priced. The four-sentence proof conceals seven, one of them an entire missing Mathlib theorem hiding behind the single word “with”.
  2. 02 The proof, architected for reading. The order-carrying map is decoupled from the weights, so dualization is free and the theorem comes out strictly stronger than the problem it came from: classical Erdős–Szekeres falls out as the unit-weight corollary.
  3. 03 A failure atlas for the automation. Root causes wherever tactics or the library fell short, hard subgoals extracted into a standalone benchmark set for provers, and three concrete Mathlib gaps surfaced.

The two proofs are morally the same argument, yet one costs three to four times the other to formalize. In each, the step the informal text spends the fewest words on is the most expensive one.

/-- Weighted Erdős–Szekeres, ℓ² form. -/
theorem sum_sq_le_sq_maxMonoSum
    (v : Fin n → β) (w : Fin n → ℝ)
    (hv : Function.Injective v) (hw : ∀ i, 0 < w i) :
    ∑ i, w i ^ 2 ≤ maxMonoSum v w ^ 2

with v = w = x this is erdős #1026 · with w ≡ 1 it is classical erdős–szekeres

∀ § 02 prooftree
∀ ProofTree The place where mathematics gets done.

A proof lives in five places before it is finished: the argument in a chat thread, the question on a forum, the Lean in a repository, the write-up in Overleaf, and the actual reasons in somebody’s head. The result survives. The reasoning evaporates.

ProofTree holds the statement, the drafts, the Lean and the discussion in one persistent object, with every step checked by the kernel, so the reasoning compounds instead of evaporating. Co-founded with Carl Jontza and Yola Zhou; Yang-Hui He advises. I work on the proof-state layer:

  1. 01 Content-addressed proof states. Tier, verified and source kept as three independent dimensions of a state instead of one collapsed hash.
  2. 02 A frozen syntactic canonicaliser. α-renaming, hypothesis and multi-goal sorting, v1:<sha256> addressing. Definitional equivalence is a verified edge between states, never a silent merge.
  3. 03 A drift checker, in progress. Whether AI-generated Lean is faithful to its informal source, not merely compilable.
  4. 04 LeanDojo Benchmark 4 instrumentation. The raw surface form of proof states: exact codepoints, notation, provenance.

Some arguments are larger than any one head. The Langlands program is a conversation sixty years old, distributed across thousands of people who mostly never met, coordinated by journals and seminars: instruments older than the telephone. When the medium that holds an argument changes, what a field can attempt changes with it. That is the bet.

∴ § 04 how i got here

I was certain about mathematics long before I was certain about anything else. The subject was never in question; the question was which mathematical life to live, and I was bad at choosing. Belfast for the degree, Bonn for number theory, and a number of half-started futures in between.

AI arrived as a toy, not a plan. I used it for fun, then to do mathematics faster, then to map exactly where it fails. Meanwhile the people I knew from the mathematical world, the ones who run the seminars and keep the problem lists, began asking the same question from the other side: what happens to mathematics when proofs get cheap. ProofTree is where the two lines crossed.
