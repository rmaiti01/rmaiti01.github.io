∀ ProofTree

The place where mathematics gets done.
Y Combinator AI Startup School ’26 Microsoft for Startups Renaissance Philanthropy shortlist

A proof lives in five places at once: a chat thread, a forum question, a Lean repository, an Overleaf draft, and the real reasons in somebody’s head. The result survives. The reasoning evaporates.

ProofTree keeps one persistent object per proof: statement, drafts, Lean and discussion together, every step checked by the kernel. For mathematicians, it is the first place the whole proof lives in one object. For AI labs, the byproduct is the scarce input: live, verified human and machine reasoning traces, the kind of record that almost never survives into print.

Three founders, all mathematicians, between Bonn and Munich. Yang-Hui He of the London Institute for Mathematical Sciences advises. My work is the proof-state layer:

  1. 01 Content-addressed proof states. Tier, verified and source kept as three independent dimensions of a state instead of one collapsed hash.
  2. 02 A frozen syntactic canonicaliser. α-renaming, hypothesis and multi-goal sorting, v1:<sha256> addressing. Definitional equivalence is recorded as a verified edge between states, so nothing merges silently.
  3. 03 A drift checker, in progress. Whether AI-generated Lean still says what the informal text said. Compiling is the easy half.
  4. 04 LeanDojo Benchmark 4 instrumentation. The raw surface form of proof states: exact codepoints, notation, provenance.

Some arguments are larger than any one head. The Langlands program is a conversation sixty years old, running across thousands of people who mostly never met, coordinated by journals and seminars: instruments older than the telephone. When the medium that holds an argument changes, what a field can attempt changes with it. That is the bet. It also happens to be the business: whoever hosts the reasoning holds training data no lab can buy.

∎ § 02 the artifact

Erdős #1026, by hand.

What the four sentences were hiding.

The problem both solvers settled is Cambie’s conjecture, open since 1995:

erdős problem #1026 If x1, …, xk² are distinct positive reals with x1 + ⋯ + xk² = 1, some monotone subsequence has sum at least 1/k.

The AI prover Aristotle settled it first, autonomously, in 3,658 generated lines of Lean. Koishi Chan, a human reading the same problem list, posted four sentences an hour later. A small-world footnote: that list is erdosproblems.com, kept by Thomas Bloom, who three years earlier supervised my research summer at Oxford.

Most people read the episode as a story about machines. I read both proofs the way a referee would, then formalized the theorem the way a person would write it: about 330 lines of structured Lean 4. Zero sorries, CI on every push, kernel-checked axiom audit. The Lean is the receipt. The deliverable is the reading around it:

  1. 01 A referee’s report on both informal proofs. Every gap, hidden assumption and unstated lemma, flagged and priced. The four-sentence proof conceals seven, one of them an entire missing Mathlib theorem hiding behind the single word “with”.
  2. 02 The proof, architected for reading. The order-carrying map is decoupled from the weights, so dualization is free and the theorem comes out strictly stronger than the problem it came from: classical Erdős–Szekeres falls out as the unit-weight corollary.
  3. 03 A failure atlas for the automation. Root causes wherever tactics or the library fell short, hard subgoals extracted into a standalone benchmark set for provers, and three concrete Mathlib gaps surfaced.

The two proofs are morally the same argument, yet one costs three to four times the other to formalize. In each, the step the informal text spends the fewest words on is the most expensive one.

/-- Weighted Erdős–Szekeres, ℓ² form. -/
theorem sum_sq_le_sq_maxMonoSum
    (v : Fin n → β) (w : Fin n → ℝ)
    (hv : Function.Injective v) (hw : ∀ i, 0 < w i) :
    ∑ i, w i ^ 2 ≤ maxMonoSum v w ^ 2

with v = w = x this is erdős #1026 · with w ≡ 1 it is classical erdős–szekeres

ζ § 03 mathematics

Number theory, leaning analytic.

Oxford, 2022, was a research summer with Thomas Bloom: gaps between sums of two squares. Thomas now keeps erdosproblems.com, the problem list at the center of the AI prover moment. The corner of mathematics I grew up in turned out to be the one the machines visited first.

The summer before, special values of zeta and L-functions with Tiago Jardim da Fonseca. At Bonn: the smooth circle method after Heath-Brown, presented as a graduate seminar, and the Logic of Proof Assistants with Floris van Doorn, where the type theory underneath Lean stopped being folklore and became coursework.

And Yang-Hui He, who was teaching machines mathematics years before it was fashionable, advises ProofTree and keeps an open line of work with me: whether the optimisation problems inside the circle method are amenable to machine learning. Early, and labeled as such.

∴ § 04 how i got here

I loved mathematics long before I could prove it on paper. I am neurodivergent, and for years my grades said one thing while every spare hour said another. What I had instead was luck and a habit of ending up in the right rooms: a research summer at Oxford, and then, somehow, Bonn.

Bonn is a humbling place to love this subject. Fields medalists in the corridors, a century of mathematics in the walls, peers who are frighteningly quick. I struggled there and I learnt there, in that order. It is where the obsession finally became something I could build with, and where I met one of my cofounders.

AI arrived as a toy, not a plan. I used it for fun, then to do mathematics faster, then to map exactly where it fails. Somewhere in that mapping the two threads lined up. The ambition I had never managed to narrow down turned out to be the right size for exactly one thing: building the field itself. ProofTree is that, with a kernel checking every line of it.
